# Shark Facts: Shark Conservation, Threats, and Finning

Sharks have patrolled the world's oceans for more than four hundred million years, surviving multiple mass extinctions that wiped out countless other forms of life. Yet in the span of just a few decades, human activity has pushed many shark species toward the brink of extinction. Scientists estimate that populations of many oceanic sharks have declined dramatically since the middle of the twentieth century, and a substantial fraction of shark and ray species are now considered threatened. Understanding the causes of this decline and the measures being taken to address it is essential to preserving these ancient and ecologically vital animals.

The single greatest threat to sharks is overfishing. Tens of millions of sharks are killed each year, and by some estimates the annual toll reaches into the range of a hundred million animals when both targeted catches and accidental capture are counted. Sharks are caught for their meat, their fins, their liver oil, their cartilage, and their skin. Because sharks grow slowly, mature late, and produce few young, they cannot reproduce quickly enough to replace the numbers removed by intensive fishing. A population that is heavily fished can collapse within years, and recovery, when it happens at all, may take decades.

Among the most controversial and damaging practices is shark finning. In finning, fishers catch a shark, cut off its valuable fins, and discard the still-living body back into the sea, where the animal, unable to swim, sinks and dies. This practice is driven by the high value of shark fins, which are used primarily to make shark fin soup, a traditional delicacy in some cultures where it is regarded as a luxury and a symbol of status. Because the fins are far more valuable than the meat, finning allows vessels to maximize profit while using minimal storage space, resulting in the deaths of enormous numbers of sharks for only a small portion of each animal.

The scale of the fin trade is staggering. The fins of tens of millions of sharks pass through global markets each year. Because finning is so wasteful and cruel, and because it contributes heavily to population declines, many countries and regional fishery bodies have enacted laws against it. A common approach is the fins-attached rule, which requires that sharks be landed with their fins still naturally attached to their bodies. This regulation discourages finning by removing the incentive to discard carcasses at sea and makes enforcement easier, since inspectors can readily identify illegally finned catches.

Beyond targeted fishing and finning, sharks face a serious threat from bycatch, the accidental capture of non-target species in fishing gear. Longlines set for tuna and swordfish, gillnets, and trawls entangle and kill large numbers of sharks that were never the intended catch. Because bycatch is incidental, it is difficult to regulate and often goes underreported, yet it may account for a large share of total shark mortality. Some fisheries have adopted modified gear, such as circle hooks and specialized nets, and time-and-area closures to reduce the accidental capture of sharks and other vulnerable animals.

Habitat degradation compounds these pressures. Coastal development, pollution, and the destruction of mangroves, seagrass beds, and coral reefs damage the shallow nursery areas where many shark species give birth and where their young spend their vulnerable early years. Without healthy nurseries, even sharks that survive to adulthood may fail to produce a new generation. Climate change adds further stress by warming ocean waters, altering currents, shifting the distribution of prey, and changing the chemistry of the sea, all of which can disrupt shark behavior, migration, and reproduction.

The consequences of losing sharks extend far beyond the animals themselves. As apex and mesopredators, sharks occupy the upper levels of marine food webs and help regulate the populations of the species below them. By removing sick and weak individuals and controlling the numbers of mid-level predators, sharks help maintain the balance and diversity of marine ecosystems. When shark populations collapse, the effects can ripple through the food web in a process called a trophic cascade. For example, the decline of large sharks along some coastlines has been linked to increases in the rays and smaller sharks they once ate, which in turn have devastated shellfish populations and the fisheries that depend on them.

Recognizing these dangers, governments and international bodies have taken a range of conservation actions. Many countries have banned finning, prohibited the fishing of particularly vulnerable species, and established marine protected areas and shark sanctuaries where fishing is restricted or banned entirely. Several nations have declared their entire exclusive economic zones to be shark sanctuaries, protecting sharks across vast areas of ocean. International agreements regulate the trade in the most threatened species, requiring that exports be legal and sustainable, and adding species to protected lists helps curb the global trade in fins and other products.

Scientific research and monitoring underpin these efforts. Researchers use satellite and acoustic tags to track shark movements, genetic tools to identify populations and trace the origin of traded fins, and population surveys to assess the health of stocks. This information helps managers set catch limits, identify critical habitats, and evaluate whether protections are working. Because many sharks migrate across national boundaries, effective conservation requires cooperation among countries that share the same populations, making international collaboration a cornerstone of shark protection.

Public attitudes toward sharks are also changing, and this shift is itself an important conservation tool. For decades, sharks were portrayed as mindless monsters, a reputation reinforced by sensational films and news coverage of the rare attacks on humans. In reality, the number of people killed by sharks each year is tiny, far smaller than the number of sharks killed by people in a matter of minutes. As the public has come to appreciate the ecological importance and the vulnerability of sharks, demand for shark fin soup has begun to fall in some markets, and shark-based ecotourism, in which divers pay to observe living sharks, has demonstrated that a live shark can be far more valuable than a dead one.

The future of sharks depends on sustaining and strengthening these efforts. Reducing overfishing and bycatch, enforcing bans on finning, protecting nursery habitats, combating illegal trade, and continuing to shift public perception are all necessary to reverse the decline. Sharks have survived for hundreds of millions of years, but their survival through the coming century will depend on the choices that people make now. Protecting sharks means protecting the health and resilience of the oceans themselves, on which countless other species, including humans, ultimately depend.
