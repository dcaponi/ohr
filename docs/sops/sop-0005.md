# Standard Operating Procedure: Analytical Balance Weighing

## Purpose

This standard operating procedure describes the correct use of an analytical balance to measure mass with high precision, typically to a resolution of 0.1 milligram or finer. Analytical balances are used to prepare standards and reagents, to determine the mass of samples for quantitative analysis, and to verify volumetric measurements gravimetrically. Because these instruments are extraordinarily sensitive, small environmental disturbances such as air currents, vibration, temperature gradients, and static charge can introduce significant error. This procedure describes how to level, calibrate, and operate the balance so that measurements are accurate, reproducible, and traceable.

## Safety

Although weighing is a low-hazard activity, the substances being weighed may be hazardous, so handle chemicals with the appropriate gloves and eye protection and consult the relevant safety data sheets. Never weigh volatile, reactive, or dusty hazardous materials directly in the open balance chamber without suitable containment, and clean spills immediately to protect both the operator and the delicate weighing mechanism. Avoid weighing very cold or very hot objects, since temperature differences create convection currents that disturb the reading and condensation that corrodes internal parts. Keep the balance away from fume hoods, doorways, and ventilation outlets that create air movement.

## Materials

You will need the analytical balance on a stable, vibration-free bench, certified calibration weights traceable to a recognized standard, clean weighing vessels such as weigh boats or tared containers, a soft brush or anti-static device for cleaning the pan and chamber, forceps or gloves for handling calibration weights, and the calibration and use log.

## Procedure

Confirm that the balance is on a heavy, level bench isolated from vibration and away from drafts and direct sunlight. Check the built-in spirit level and adjust the leveling feet until the bubble is centered, because a balance that is out of level will not weigh accurately. Allow the instrument to warm up and stabilize for the period recommended by the manufacturer, often 30 minutes or more, before making critical measurements.

Verify calibration before a weighing session. Many balances offer internal automatic calibration, which you should initiate according to the instrument instructions. For external calibration, use certified reference weights handled only with forceps or gloves to avoid transferring oils and moisture, and confirm that the displayed mass matches the nominal value within tolerance. Record the calibration check in the log.

Before weighing, ensure the chamber doors are closed and press the tare or zero control so the display reads zero. Open a side door rather than the front door to minimize disturbance, place the empty weighing vessel on the center of the pan, close the door, and allow the reading to stabilize. Tare the balance again to zero out the vessel mass.

Add the sample to the vessel in small increments, closing the draft shield each time before reading. Wait for the stability indicator before recording the mass, and never lean on the bench or touch the balance while the reading settles. When transferring a target mass of reagent, dispense slowly and observe the display, because overshooting a hygroscopic or fine powder is difficult to correct. Record the final mass promptly and directly into your notebook or connected data system.

When finished, remove the vessel, close the doors, and return the display to zero for the next user.

## Maintenance and Storage

Keep the weighing chamber scrupulously clean. Brush away spilled material with a soft brush after each use, taking care not to bump the pan, and address any spill immediately to prevent corrosion or contamination of future samples. Periodically remove the pan and clean beneath it following the manufacturer instructions. Use an anti-static ionizer when weighing plastic vessels or powders that carry static charge, since static forces produce unstable and inaccurate readings. Schedule routine servicing and external calibration by a qualified technician at defined intervals, and retain the calibration certificates for traceability. Leave the balance powered and closed between uses so that it remains thermally equilibrated, and cover it or store it in a clean, dry environment free from corrosive vapors and vibration.
