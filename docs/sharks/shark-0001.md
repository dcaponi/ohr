# Shark Facts: Great White Shark Biology and Behavior

The great white shark, Carcharodon carcharias, is among the most recognizable and studied predators in the ocean. It belongs to the family Lamnidae, the mackerel sharks, a group that also includes the mako and porbeagle sharks. Great whites are found in cool and temperate coastal waters throughout the world, with well-known populations off the coasts of South Africa, southern Australia, California, the northeastern United States, and the Mediterranean. They are highly migratory and capable of crossing entire ocean basins, a behavior that researchers have documented using satellite tags.

Adult great white sharks are large animals. Most mature individuals measure between four and six meters in length, and the largest reliably measured specimens approach or slightly exceed six meters. Mature females tend to grow larger than males, a pattern common among sharks. A large adult can weigh well over a thousand kilograms. The species grows slowly and matures late, with females often not reaching reproductive maturity until they are more than thirty years old. Individuals may live seventy years or more, making the great white one of the longest-lived cartilaginous fishes known.

The body of the great white is built for efficient, powerful swimming. It has a robust, torpedo-shaped body, a conical snout, and a crescent-shaped tail that provides thrust. The coloration gives the animal its name: the underside is white while the dorsal surface is a slate gray, a countershading pattern that camouflages the shark from both above and below. When viewed from beneath against the bright surface, its pale belly blends with the light, and when viewed from above against the dark depths, its gray back disappears. This concealment is a crucial advantage for an ambush predator.

One of the most remarkable features of the great white is its ability to maintain a body temperature warmer than the surrounding water. Like other lamnid sharks, it is regionally endothermic, using a specialized network of blood vessels called the rete mirabile to conserve metabolic heat. This countercurrent heat exchanger keeps the swimming muscles, stomach, brain, and eyes warmer than the ambient sea. Warm muscles contract more quickly and powerfully, allowing the shark to accelerate rapidly during an attack and to remain active in cold water where many other predators would be sluggish.

The teeth of the great white are large, triangular, and serrated along their edges. Unlike mammalian teeth, shark teeth are not anchored in sockets but are arranged in rows that continuously advance as older teeth are lost. A great white may replace thousands of teeth over its lifetime. The serrated edges act like a saw, allowing the shark to shear through the blubber and flesh of large prey. The jaws themselves are only loosely attached to the skull and can be projected forward during a bite, increasing the gape and the force delivered to prey.

Great whites are apex predators with a varied diet that changes as they grow. Juveniles feed mainly on fish, rays, and smaller sharks, because their jaws and teeth are not yet strong enough to handle large marine mammals. As they mature, their diet shifts toward energy-rich prey such as seals, sea lions, and small cetaceans. This dietary transition is reflected in the changing shape of their teeth, which become broader and more suited to cutting through thick blubber. The high caloric value of marine mammal blubber helps fuel the shark's warm-bodied metabolism.

Hunting behavior in great whites is sophisticated and often relies on stealth. Off the coast of South Africa, great whites are famous for their predatory strikes on Cape fur seals near seal colonies. A shark will patrol the darker water near the seabed, using its countershading to remain hidden, then rush upward with tremendous speed to strike a seal at the surface. These attacks are sometimes so forceful that the shark, and its prey, briefly launch clear of the water, a behavior known as breaching. The initial strike is typically a powerful bite intended to wound and disable the prey, after which the shark may wait for the animal to weaken before consuming it.

The senses of the great white are exceptionally well developed and support its role as a hunter. Its sense of smell is acute, able to detect small concentrations of blood and bodily fluids carried on currents. Its eyes are adapted for both bright and dim conditions. Along its snout are the ampullae of Lorenzini, electroreceptive organs that detect the faint electrical fields produced by the muscle movements of living animals, allowing the shark to locate prey even in murky water or when it is hidden. A lateral line running along the body senses vibrations and pressure changes, helping the shark detect movement at a distance.

Despite their fearsome reputation, great white sharks are not the mindless man-eaters of popular imagination. Attacks on humans are rare, and many bites are believed to be exploratory rather than predatory, with the shark releasing the person after a single bite once it recognizes that a human is not its usual, fat-rich prey. Humans are simply not a nutritionally suitable target for an animal adapted to feeding on blubber-laden marine mammals. Nonetheless, the size and power of the great white mean that even an exploratory bite can be extremely dangerous.

The social and reproductive lives of great whites remain only partially understood, because so much of their behavior takes place far from observers. Researchers have documented apparent social structures at aggregation sites, where individuals may establish loose dominance relationships that reduce conflict. Great whites are believed to be ovoviviparous, with embryos developing inside the mother and hatching internally before live birth. Developing pups practice a form of intrauterine feeding, consuming unfertilized eggs produced by the mother, a behavior called oophagy. Litters are small, typically numbering a handful of large, well-developed pups that are immediately independent at birth.

The conservation status of the great white shark is a matter of concern. Their slow growth, late maturity, and small litters make populations vulnerable to overexploitation, because animals removed from the population cannot be replaced quickly. Great whites are threatened by targeted fishing, accidental capture in nets and longlines, and the demand for their jaws, teeth, and fins. Recognizing these pressures, many countries have granted the species legal protection, prohibiting targeted fishing and trade. Because they are apex predators, great whites help maintain the balance of marine ecosystems by regulating the populations of the animals they prey upon, and their decline can have cascading effects throughout the food web.

Continued research using satellite tags, underwater cameras, and genetic sampling has steadily improved our understanding of this iconic species. Scientists have discovered long-distance migrations, mysterious deep-water diving behavior, and the existence of distinct regional populations that rarely interbreed. Far from being simple killing machines, great white sharks are revealed to be complex, intelligent, and ecologically vital animals whose survival is closely tied to the health of the world's oceans.
