# Shark Facts: Shark Reproduction and Life Cycle

Sharks display one of the most diverse and fascinating ranges of reproductive strategies found among vertebrates. Unlike most bony fishes, which release enormous numbers of tiny eggs and sperm into the water in the hope that a few will survive, sharks invest heavily in a small number of large, well-developed offspring. This strategy of producing few but capable young shapes nearly every aspect of shark reproduction, from internal fertilization to the long gestation periods and slow population growth that make sharks so vulnerable to overfishing.

All sharks reproduce through internal fertilization, a relatively advanced strategy among fishes. Male sharks possess a pair of organs called claspers, which are modified extensions of the pelvic fins. During mating, the male inserts one clasper into the female's cloaca to transfer sperm. Courtship and mating can be vigorous and even rough, and in many species the male bites and holds the female during copulation. For this reason, females of some species have notably thicker skin than males, an adaptation that reduces injury during mating.

Once fertilization has occurred, sharks follow one of three broad reproductive modes: oviparity, viviparity, or ovoviviparity, the last of which is often described more precisely as aplacental viviparity. Each mode represents a different balance between the protection offered by the mother's body and the resources invested in each embryo. The particular strategy a species uses reflects its evolutionary history and the environment in which it lives.

Oviparous, or egg-laying, sharks deposit their fertilized eggs outside the body, each enclosed in a tough, protective capsule. These egg cases are often leathery and equipped with tendrils or spiral flanges that anchor them to seaweed, rocks, or the seabed. The empty cases that wash ashore are sometimes called mermaid's purses. Inside the case, the developing embryo is nourished by a large yolk sac and takes anywhere from several weeks to many months to develop before hatching as a miniature, fully formed shark. Because the egg case offers no parental defense, its thick walls and hidden placement are the embryo's only protection. Horn sharks, many catsharks, and some bamboo sharks reproduce this way.

Viviparous sharks, by contrast, give birth to live young that have been nourished directly by the mother through a placental connection. In these species, the yolk sac eventually attaches to the wall of the uterus and forms a structure functionally similar to the mammalian placenta. Through this connection, the mother supplies oxygen and nutrients to the developing pups and removes their waste. Placental viviparity allows the embryos to grow large and strong before birth and is found in a number of requiem sharks, including the bull shark and the blue shark. This mode represents one of the most advanced forms of maternal investment among fishes.

The third mode, aplacental viviparity, sits between the other two. In these species the eggs are retained and hatch inside the mother's body, but there is no placental connection. Instead, the embryos are nourished in other ways. In the simplest cases the embryos rely on their yolk sacs while developing within the safety of the mother's body, gaining protection without additional feeding. In more specialized cases, the mother provides extra nourishment through remarkable means.

One of the most striking forms of embryonic nutrition is oophagy, in which developing embryos consume unfertilized eggs that the mother continues to produce. The growing pups feed on this steady supply of nutrient-rich eggs, allowing them to reach a large size before birth. Lamnid sharks such as the great white and the mako use oophagy. In the sand tiger shark, embryonic nutrition takes an even more extreme form known as intrauterine cannibalism, or adelphophagy. The first embryo to develop teeth consumes its smaller siblings within the uterus, so that typically only one large, formidable pup survives in each of the two uteri. This ensures that the few young born are exceptionally large and capable of surviving on their own.

Gestation periods in sharks are often remarkably long. While some species carry their young for only a few months, many require a year or more, and a few of the longest are believed to gestate for over two years. The spiny dogfish, for instance, is thought to have one of the longest gestation periods of any vertebrate, carrying its young for up to two years. Such extended pregnancies reflect the large size and advanced development of the pups at birth, which emerge ready to hunt and fend for themselves without any parental care.

Shark litters are generally small, especially when compared with the millions of eggs released by many bony fishes. Depending on the species, a litter may consist of anywhere from a single pup to several dozen. The largest species, such as the whale shark, are exceptions and can carry hundreds of embryos at various stages of development. In most sharks, however, the number of offspring is modest, and the young are born as fully formed, self-sufficient miniatures of the adults, immediately capable of swimming and hunting.

Many sharks reach reproductive maturity late in life, sometimes not until they are well over a decade old, and in some large species not until they are several decades old. Females of many species do not reproduce every year, instead following multi-year cycles that include a resting period between pregnancies to rebuild energy reserves. Some species also exhibit reproductive migrations, traveling to specific nursery areas, often shallow, sheltered coastal waters, where they give birth. These nurseries provide young sharks with abundant food and relative safety from larger predators, including adult sharks, during their vulnerable early life.

A few shark species are even capable of a form of asexual reproduction known as parthenogenesis, in which a female produces viable offspring without any contribution from a male. This phenomenon has been documented in captivity in species such as the bonnethead and the zebra shark, sometimes in females that had been isolated from males for years. While parthenogenesis is not the normal mode of reproduction and results in offspring with reduced genetic diversity, it demonstrates the remarkable reproductive flexibility of sharks.

The combination of late maturity, long gestation, small litters, and infrequent reproduction means that shark populations grow slowly and recover only gradually from decline. When too many adults are removed by fishing, there are simply not enough reproducing individuals to replace them, and populations can collapse and take decades to rebuild, if they recover at all. Understanding the details of shark reproduction is therefore not merely an academic exercise; it is essential to designing effective conservation measures that protect breeding adults and the nursery habitats on which the next generation depends.
