# Shark Facts: The Diversity of Sharks

Sharks are far more diverse than their popular image suggests. Although films and headlines tend to focus on a few large, toothy predators, the sharks are in fact a sprawling group of more than five hundred known species that vary enormously in size, shape, habitat, and behavior. They range from giants longer than a bus to dwarf species that could rest in the palm of a hand, and they inhabit every ocean on Earth, from sunlit coral reefs to the crushing darkness of the deep sea. Exploring this diversity reveals how adaptable and successful the shark body plan has proven over hundreds of millions of years.

The largest fish in the sea is a shark: the whale shark, Rhincodon typus. Despite its enormous size, reaching lengths of twelve meters or more, the whale shark is a gentle filter feeder that poses no threat to humans. It cruises slowly through warm tropical waters with its cavernous mouth open, straining plankton, small fish, and fish eggs from the water. Its skin is marked with a distinctive pattern of pale spots and stripes that is unique to each individual, much like a fingerprint, allowing researchers to identify and track particular animals over time. Whale sharks are known to gather in large numbers at seasonal feeding sites where plankton or fish spawn is abundant.

A second giant filter feeder is the basking shark, Cetorhinus maximus, the second-largest fish in the world. Like the whale shark, it feeds passively by swimming with its mouth agape, but it strains plankton from cold, temperate waters using long gill rakers. Basking sharks are often seen slowly circling near the surface, their great dorsal fins cutting through the water, a sight that has occasionally been mistaken for something more menacing. These sluggish giants undertake long migrations and can dive to considerable depths in search of the dense plankton patches on which they depend.

Among the most instantly recognizable sharks are the hammerheads, distinguished by the flattened, blade-like extension of the head known as the cephalofoil. This peculiar shape is not merely decorative. The wide head spreads the shark's sensory organs, including its nostrils and the electroreceptive ampullae of Lorenzini, over a broader area, improving its ability to detect prey buried in the sand. The eyes positioned at the ends of the cephalofoil give hammerheads an exceptionally wide field of view. Hammerheads use these adaptations to hunt rays, one of their favored prey, sometimes pinning a ray to the seabed with their broad heads. The great hammerhead is the largest of the group, while smaller relatives such as the bonnethead frequent shallow coastal waters.

For sheer speed, few animals can match the shortfin mako, Isurus oxyrinchus, widely regarded as the fastest shark in the sea. A member of the same family as the great white, the mako is built like a torpedo, with a slender, muscular body and a stiff, crescent-shaped tail. Like its lamnid relatives, it is regionally warm-bodied, keeping its swimming muscles hotter than the surrounding water, which allows explosive bursts of speed to chase down fast prey such as tuna and squid. Makos are capable of leaping high out of the water, a behavior that makes them prized, and unfortunately heavily targeted, by anglers.

At the opposite end of the temperament scale lies the nurse shark, Ginglymostoma cirratum, a slow-moving, bottom-dwelling species common on shallow reefs and in mangrove channels of warm coastal waters. Nurse sharks spend much of the day resting motionless on the seabed, sometimes piled together in groups, and become active at night. They feed by suction, using their small mouths to vacuum up crustaceans, mollusks, and small fish from the bottom, and they possess sensory barbels near the nostrils to help locate hidden prey. Their docile nature makes them a favorite subject for divers, though like all sharks they will bite if provoked.

The deep sea harbors some of the strangest sharks of all. The cookiecutter shark is a small species that uses its suction-cup lips and circular rows of teeth to gouge round plugs of flesh from much larger animals, including whales, tuna, and even the occasional submarine cable. The frilled shark and the goblin shark are eel-like and bizarre in appearance, relics of ancient lineages that survive in the deep. The goblin shark, with its protrusible jaws and long, blade-like snout, is sometimes called a living fossil. The Greenland shark, a slow-moving inhabitant of frigid Arctic and deep waters, is thought to be among the longest-lived vertebrates on Earth, with some individuals estimated to be several centuries old.

Not all sharks are large. The dwarf lanternshark and other diminutive deep-sea species measure only about twenty centimeters in length, and many produce their own light through bioluminescence. Light-emitting organs called photophores dot the undersides of these small sharks, helping them blend with the faint light filtering down from above, a form of camouflage known as counterillumination, and possibly helping them communicate or attract prey in the dark depths. These tiny, glowing sharks are a reminder of how varied the group truly is.

Other sharks display remarkable specializations for their particular ways of life. The angel sharks are flattened, ray-like ambush predators that bury themselves in sand and lie in wait for passing prey. Wobbegongs, or carpet sharks, are masters of camouflage, their mottled skin and fringed mouths blending perfectly with the reef as they lie in wait. Thresher sharks wield an enormously elongated upper tail lobe, nearly as long as the rest of the body, which they use like a whip to stun schooling fish before eating them. The sawsharks bear a long, tooth-studded snout that they use to slash at prey. Each of these forms represents a different solution to the challenge of finding and capturing food.

Sharks belong to a larger group of cartilaginous fishes, the chondrichthyans, which also includes the rays, skates, and the strange, deep-water chimaeras. Rays and skates are, in an evolutionary sense, essentially flattened relatives of sharks, and the boundary between the two groups is blurred by intermediate forms such as the guitarfishes and sawfishes. This shared ancestry underlies the many anatomical features that sharks and rays have in common, from their cartilaginous skeletons to their electroreceptive senses.

The extraordinary diversity of sharks reflects their long evolutionary history and their success in colonizing nearly every marine environment. From plankton-sieving giants to bioluminescent dwarfs, from lightning-fast open-ocean hunters to sluggish bottom-dwellers and deep-sea oddities, sharks have evolved a dazzling array of forms and lifestyles. Appreciating this diversity is important not only for its own sake but for conservation, because protecting sharks means safeguarding not just a handful of famous predators but an entire, ancient, and irreplaceable branch of life in the sea.
