# Standard Operating Procedure: Compound Light Microscope Operation

## Purpose

This standard operating procedure describes the correct use of a compound brightfield light microscope for observing prepared specimens at magnifications typically ranging from 40 to 1000 times. A compound microscope uses an illuminator, a condenser, an objective lens, and an ocular lens working together to produce a magnified, resolved image of a thin, translucent sample. Correct technique preserves the delicate optics, produces sharp and evenly illuminated images, and prevents damage to both slides and objectives. This procedure covers startup, focusing through the objective series, adjustment of illumination using Koehler alignment principles, and shutdown.

## Safety

Although a light microscope is a low-hazard instrument, several precautions protect both the user and the equipment. The illuminator bulb becomes hot during use and should be allowed to cool before handling or replacement. Never look directly into a high-intensity illuminator, and reduce the lamp brightness to its lowest setting before switching the instrument on or off to prolong bulb life. When using immersion oil, use only the grade specified for your objectives, because incorrect oil can damage lens coatings. If specimens are biological and potentially infectious, treat slides as contaminated, handle them with care to avoid cuts from broken glass, and disinfect the stage after use. Carry the microscope with one hand on the arm and one hand supporting the base.

## Materials

You will need the compound microscope, a prepared and properly mounted specimen slide with a coverslip, lens cleaning tissue and an approved lens cleaning solution, immersion oil if you intend to use the highest-power objective, and a dust cover for storage. Keep a logbook nearby to record lamp hours and any maintenance.

## Procedure

Place the microscope on a stable bench and connect it to power. Rotate the nosepiece so that the lowest-power objective, usually the 4x scanning objective, clicks into position over the stage. Turn on the illuminator and set the brightness to a comfortable, moderate level. Place the slide on the stage with the coverslip facing up and secure it with the stage clips, then use the stage controls to center the specimen over the light aperture.

While observing from the side, use the coarse focus knob to bring the objective and the slide close together without allowing them to touch. Then look through the oculars and slowly turn the coarse focus knob to increase the working distance until the specimen comes into approximate focus. Refine the image with the fine focus knob. Adjust the interpupillary distance of the oculars so that the two fields merge into a single circular image, and set any diopter adjustment to accommodate differences between your eyes.

Optimize the illumination by partially closing the field diaphragm and adjusting the condenser height until the edges of the diaphragm image appear sharp in the field of view, then reopen the diaphragm until it just clears the field. This Koehler alignment produces even, glare-free lighting. Adjust the condenser aperture diaphragm to balance contrast and resolution rather than using it to control brightness.

To increase magnification, rotate the next higher-power objective into place. Because the objectives are approximately parfocal, only small fine-focus adjustments should be needed. Never use the coarse focus knob with high-power objectives, as doing so can drive the lens into the slide. For oil immersion at the highest power, place a single drop of immersion oil on the coverslip over the specimen, rotate the oil objective into the oil while avoiding the dry objectives, and focus using only the fine focus knob.

When you finish, rotate the lowest-power objective back into position, remove the slide, and reduce the lamp to minimum before switching off the illuminator.

## Maintenance and Storage

Clean the oculars and objectives only with approved lens tissue and lens cleaning solution, wiping in a gentle circular motion; never use ordinary paper or cloth, which scratches the coatings. Immediately after using oil, clean the oil objective and any contaminated surfaces so that residue does not harden. Wipe the stage with a mild disinfectant when biological samples were examined. Record cumulative lamp hours and replace the bulb before it fails during critical work. When finished, lower the stage, rotate the low-power objective into place, coil the power cord loosely, and cover the microscope with its dust cover. Store the instrument in a dry location away from vibration and corrosive fumes.
