# Standard Operating Procedure: Steam Autoclave Sterilization

## Purpose

This standard operating procedure describes the safe operation of a steam autoclave used to sterilize laboratory media, glassware, instruments, and biohazardous waste. An autoclave achieves sterilization by exposing its load to saturated steam under pressure, typically at 121 degrees Celsius and 15 pounds per square inch of gauge pressure for a defined hold time. Under these conditions the moist heat denatures proteins and destroys bacterial spores, viruses, and other microorganisms. This procedure ensures that loads are effectively sterilized, that operators are protected from burns and pressure hazards, and that cycles are documented for quality control.

## Safety

The autoclave combines high temperature, pressurized steam, and heavy doors, all of which present serious hazards. Superheated steam causes instant and severe burns, so always wear heat-resistant gloves, a face shield or safety glasses, and a lab coat when unloading. Never attempt to open the door until the chamber pressure has returned to zero and the temperature has dropped, because opening a pressurized chamber can cause an explosive release of steam. Liquids are especially dangerous, since removing them too soon can cause superheated media to boil over violently; allow liquid loads to cool before handling. Never autoclave sealed containers, volatile or flammable solvents, corrosives, or bleach, as these can explode, ignite, or damage the chamber. Load items loosely to allow steam penetration, and place a drip tray under liquid containers.

## Materials

You will need autoclave-safe containers and bags rated for steam sterilization, autoclave tape or chemical indicators, biological indicators for periodic validation, a secondary containment tray, heat-resistant gloves, and the instrument log. Ensure the reservoir has sufficient deionized or distilled water if the unit is not plumbed to a water supply.

## Procedure

Begin by checking that the chamber drain screen is clean and that the water reservoir is adequately filled. Prepare the load by loosening caps on bottles so pressure can equalize, venting bags so steam can penetrate, and placing items in a secondary containment tray. Apply autoclave tape or place a chemical indicator with the load so that successful exposure can be confirmed afterward. Do not overload the chamber; steam must circulate freely around every item.

Place the load into the chamber, close the door, and secure the latch fully. Select the appropriate cycle for the load type. Use a gravity cycle for glassware and unwrapped goods, and use a liquid or slow-exhaust cycle for media and other liquids so that pressure is released gradually and boil-over is prevented. Set the sterilization temperature and hold time according to your validated parameters, commonly 121 degrees Celsius for 15 to 30 minutes depending on load volume and density.

Start the cycle and confirm that the autoclave reaches and maintains the target temperature and pressure through the hold period. Do not leave a running autoclave unattended for its first cycle of the day, and remain aware of the estimated completion time.

When the cycle is complete, wait for the chamber pressure gauge to read zero and allow the temperature to fall before releasing the door latch. Crack the door open slightly and let residual steam escape for several minutes before opening it fully. Using heat-resistant gloves, remove the load carefully, keeping liquid containers within the containment tray, and set them aside to cool undisturbed.

## Maintenance and Storage

Confirm sterilization by checking that chemical indicators have changed appropriately, and record the cycle parameters, load contents, and indicator results in the instrument log. Periodically run a biological indicator containing bacterial spores to validate that the autoclave achieves true sterilization, and document the result. Clean the chamber drain screen regularly to prevent clogging by debris, which can trap air and cause cycle failures. Wipe down the interior and door gasket, and inspect the gasket for cracks or hardening that would compromise the seal. Keep the reservoir filled with distilled water to reduce scale buildup, and schedule professional inspection of the pressure vessel and safety valves at the intervals required by regulation. Leave the door slightly ajar when the autoclave is not in use to prevent gasket compression and mildew.
