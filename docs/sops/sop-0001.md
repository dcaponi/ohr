# Standard Operating Procedure: Benchtop Centrifuge Operation

## Purpose

This standard operating procedure describes the correct and safe operation of a benchtop centrifuge used to separate suspended particles from liquid samples by applying centrifugal force. Centrifugation is a routine step in sample preparation, pelleting cells, clarifying lysates, and separating phases of density-graded mixtures. The procedure applies to fixed-angle and swing-bucket rotors on microcentrifuges and general-purpose benchtop units. Following this document ensures reproducible separations, protects samples from degradation, and prevents mechanical failures that can injure personnel.

## Safety

The single greatest hazard associated with a centrifuge is rotor imbalance. An unbalanced load spinning at high speed generates enormous forces that can crack the rotor, breach the chamber, and turn tubes or fragments into high-velocity projectiles. Always load tubes symmetrically so that opposing positions carry equal masses. If you have an odd number of samples, prepare a balance tube filled with water to the same weight. Never open the lid while the rotor is still spinning, and never attempt to slow the rotor by hand. Inspect tubes for cracks before loading, because a tube that fails under load can release aerosols of the sample. When processing biological or infectious material, use sealed buckets or aerosol-tight rotor lids and open them inside a biosafety cabinet. Wear gloves, a lab coat, and safety glasses at all times.

## Materials

You will need the centrifuge with an appropriate rotor for your tube format, correctly rated centrifuge tubes with secure caps, a balance or a set of pre-weighed balance tubes, and a laboratory timer if the instrument does not provide one. Have a laboratory wipe and 70 percent ethanol available for cleaning spills, and keep the manufacturer manual accessible for rotor speed ratings and maximum load specifications.

## Procedure

Begin by confirming that the centrifuge sits on a level, stable surface and that the rotor is properly seated and locked onto the drive spindle. Verify that the rotor is rated for the speed you intend to use; exceeding the rotor's maximum rated speed is a serious hazard.

Prepare your samples in identical tubes and balance them against one another. Weigh opposing tubes on an analytical or top-loading balance and adjust the fill volume until each pair matches within the tolerance specified by the manufacturer, typically 0.1 gram or better. Load the balanced tubes into diametrically opposite positions in the rotor.

Close and secure the rotor lid or bucket seals, then close the centrifuge chamber lid until it latches. Set the required speed, expressed either in revolutions per minute or as relative centrifugal force, and set the run time. If your protocol specifies relative centrifugal force, convert it to the correct rpm for your specific rotor radius rather than assuming values transfer between instruments.

Start the run and remain nearby during the initial acceleration. Listen and watch for unusual vibration or noise, which almost always indicates an imbalance. If you observe abnormal vibration, press stop immediately, wait for the rotor to come to a complete stop, and rebalance the load before resuming.

When the run finishes, wait for the rotor to stop completely and for the lid interlock to release before opening the chamber. Remove the tubes carefully to avoid disturbing pellets or resuspending separated layers. Record the run parameters in the instrument log.

## Maintenance and Storage

After each use, wipe the rotor chambers and the interior of the centrifuge with a laboratory wipe dampened with 70 percent ethanol, and remove any condensation from refrigerated units. Periodically remove the rotor and inspect it for corrosion, cracks, or pitting, paying special attention to the tube cavities and the central bore. Corroded aluminum rotors must be retired, because internal corrosion weakens the metal and can cause catastrophic failure. Keep a maintenance log that records rotor run hours, since many manufacturers derate rotors after a defined number of cycles. Store the rotor clean and dry, and leave the centrifuge lid open when not in use to allow the chamber to dry and to prevent seal compression.
