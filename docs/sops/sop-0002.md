# Standard Operating Procedure: Agarose Gel Electrophoresis

## Purpose

This standard operating procedure describes how to separate nucleic acid fragments by size using agarose gel electrophoresis. When a voltage is applied across a gel submerged in buffer, the negatively charged phosphate backbone of DNA or RNA migrates toward the positive electrode, and the porous agarose matrix retards larger fragments more than smaller ones. The result is a size-based separation that can be visualized under ultraviolet or blue light. This procedure covers casting the gel, loading samples, running the electrophoresis, and imaging the result. It is used routinely to check the integrity of nucleic acid preparations, to verify the size of amplified products, and to confirm restriction digests.

## Safety

Several hazards accompany this procedure. The electrophoresis power supply delivers voltages that can be lethal, so never touch the buffer, electrodes, or leads while the power is on, and always confirm the supply is off before removing the tank lid. Many nucleic acid stains are mutagenic; ethidium bromide in particular is a potent mutagen and must be handled with nitrile gloves, contained in a designated area, and disposed of as hazardous waste. Safer alternative stains are available and preferred where possible. Ultraviolet transilluminators emit radiation that damages skin and eyes, so wear a face shield and UV-blocking eyewear and cover exposed skin when viewing gels. Molten agarose is hot and can cause burns and can boil over violently when superheated in a microwave, so heat it in short intervals with swirling.

## Materials

Gather agarose powder, an appropriate running buffer such as TAE or TBE, a nucleic acid stain, a casting tray with combs, an electrophoresis tank with a power supply, a microwave or hot plate for melting the agarose, loading dye, a molecular weight ladder, and your samples. You will also need a gel imaging system with an appropriate light source and a micropipette set for loading.

## Procedure

Prepare the gel by weighing agarose to the desired percentage, typically between 0.8 and 2 percent depending on the fragment sizes you wish to resolve, and dissolve it in running buffer. Heat the mixture until the agarose is fully dissolved and the solution is clear, swirling periodically and being careful of superheating. Allow the molten agarose to cool to about 55 degrees Celsius, then add the nucleic acid stain if your protocol calls for staining in the gel.

Seal the ends of the casting tray, insert the comb to form wells, and pour the molten agarose into the tray. Remove any bubbles and let the gel solidify at room temperature until it is firm and opaque. Once set, gently remove the comb and place the gel into the electrophoresis tank with the wells nearest the negative electrode, since the samples will migrate toward the positive electrode.

Fill the tank with the same running buffer used to cast the gel until the gel is just submerged. Mix each sample with loading dye, which adds density so the sample sinks into the well and provides tracking dyes that migrate at known rates. Carefully pipette each sample into a well, taking care not to puncture the bottom of the well or to overflow into neighboring lanes. Load a molecular weight ladder in at least one lane as a size reference.

Place the lid on the tank, confirming that the electrode polarity matches your gel orientation, and connect the leads to the power supply. Set the voltage, commonly around 5 to 10 volts per centimeter of gel length, and start the run. Monitor the migration of the tracking dyes and stop the run before the leading dye front runs off the end of the gel.

Turn off and disconnect the power supply, then transfer the gel to the imaging system. Visualize the separated bands under the appropriate light source and capture an image for your records, comparing sample bands against the ladder to estimate fragment sizes.

## Maintenance and Storage

After the run, dispose of the used running buffer and the gel according to your institution's hazardous waste rules, since both may contain the nucleic acid stain. Rinse the tank, casting tray, and combs with deionized water and allow them to dry; avoid abrasive cleaning that scratches the acrylic. Inspect the electrodes and leads for corrosion or fraying and replace damaged components. Keep the transilluminator surface clean and free of dried buffer, and log any lamp replacements. Store buffers in labeled, dated containers and prepare fresh working buffer regularly, because repeated use degrades buffering capacity and alters migration.
