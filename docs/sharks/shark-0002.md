# Shark Facts: Shark Senses and Anatomy

Sharks are among the most sensory-rich animals in the sea, equipped with a suite of specialized organs that allow them to perceive their environment in ways far beyond human experience. Over more than four hundred million years of evolution, sharks have refined senses of smell, hearing, sight, touch, vibration detection, and even electroreception. Together these systems make sharks formidable hunters capable of locating prey across great distances and in near-total darkness. Understanding shark anatomy reveals how these remarkable predators are constructed and how each sense contributes to their success.

The skeleton of a shark is made not of bone but of cartilage, the same flexible, resilient tissue found in the human nose and ears. Cartilage is lighter than bone, which helps a shark maintain buoyancy without the heavy mineralized skeleton of bony fishes. It is also more flexible, allowing the tight turns and sudden bursts of speed that characterize shark movement. In some regions, particularly the jaws and vertebral column, the cartilage is reinforced with calcium deposits that add strength where it is most needed. Because cartilage rarely fossilizes well, much of what we know about ancient sharks comes from their teeth, which are made of hard, durable material.

One of the most distinctive features of shark anatomy is the skin, which is covered in tiny tooth-like structures called dermal denticles, or placoid scales. Unlike the flat, overlapping scales of bony fish, dermal denticles are shaped like small teeth, complete with an enamel-like surface and a pulp cavity, and they point toward the tail. This arrangement gives shark skin the texture of sandpaper. The denticles reduce drag and turbulence as water flows over the body, allowing the shark to swim quietly and efficiently. They also form a protective armor against parasites and injury. Engineers have studied denticle structure to design more efficient surfaces for swimsuits, boat hulls, and aircraft.

The sense of smell is perhaps the most famous of shark senses. Water enters paired nostrils, called nares, located on the underside of the snout, and flows over folded sensory tissue rich in olfactory receptors. Sharks do not use their nostrils for breathing; the nares serve smell alone. They are extraordinarily sensitive, able to detect certain chemicals at concentrations of only a few parts per billion. By comparing the timing and intensity of scent arriving at each nostril, a shark can determine the direction of an odor source and steer toward it, following a chemical trail across the water.

Closely related to smell is the sense of taste, which sharks use at close range. Taste receptors line the mouth and throat, and a shark often takes an exploratory bite of an unfamiliar object to assess whether it is suitable food. This is one reason many shark bites on humans and inedible objects end after a single bite, as the shark rejects a target that does not taste like its normal prey. Taste therefore acts as a final check before a shark commits to consuming something.

Vision in sharks is well adapted to the underwater world. Shark eyes contain both rod and cone cells, giving them good sensitivity in dim light as well as some ability to distinguish detail. Behind the retina lies a reflective layer called the tapetum lucidum, made of mirror-like crystals that bounce light back through the retina, effectively doubling the chance that a photon is detected. This adaptation greatly enhances vision in the dark or murky depths and is responsible for the eyeshine seen when light strikes a shark's eye. Many sharks can also protect their eyes during an attack, either by rolling the eye backward or by drawing a protective membrane, the nictitating membrane, across the eye.

Hearing is a subtle but important sense for sharks. They possess inner ears located within the cartilage of the skull, without external ear openings. These inner ears detect low-frequency sounds and vibrations that travel efficiently through water, such as the irregular thrashing of a wounded fish. Sharks are particularly attuned to low, pulsing sounds, which may signal struggling prey, and can orient toward such sounds from considerable distances.

The lateral line is a sensory system shared with bony fishes and is central to a shark's perception of movement. It consists of a network of fluid-filled canals running along each side of the body and over the head, lined with tiny hair cells that detect changes in water pressure and motion. Through the lateral line, a shark can feel the wake left by a swimming animal, sense the approach of another creature, and detect turbulence generated by its own passage near objects. This distant-touch sense allows sharks to navigate and hunt effectively even when vision is limited.

Perhaps the most extraordinary shark sense is electroreception. Scattered across the snout and head are hundreds of small pores that lead to jelly-filled canals ending in sensory cells. These structures are the ampullae of Lorenzini, named for the anatomist who first described them. Every living animal produces weak electrical fields as its muscles and nerves work, and the ampullae of Lorenzini are sensitive enough to detect these faint bioelectric signals. Using this sense, a shark can locate prey hidden beneath sand, find animals in complete darkness, and detect the beating heart of a motionless fish. Some researchers believe sharks may also use the ampullae to sense the Earth's magnetic field, aiding in long-distance navigation.

The respiratory anatomy of sharks centers on the gills, which extract dissolved oxygen from seawater. Most sharks have five pairs of gill slits, though some species have six or seven. Water passes over the gill filaments, where oxygen diffuses into the blood and carbon dioxide diffuses out. Many active sharks rely on ram ventilation, forcing water over the gills by swimming forward with their mouths open, which is why some species must keep moving to breathe. Other, more sedentary sharks can actively pump water over their gills using muscles, allowing them to rest on the seabed. Behind the eyes of some bottom-dwelling species lies a small opening called a spiracle, which draws in clean water for respiration even while the mouth is buried in sediment.

Buoyancy in sharks is managed largely by a very large, oil-rich liver rather than the gas-filled swim bladder used by bony fishes. The liver can make up a substantial fraction of a shark's body weight and is filled with low-density oils that help offset the animal's tendency to sink. Combined with the lift generated by the pectoral fins and the shape of the body, this oily liver allows sharks to control their position in the water column with relatively little effort. Together, this integrated anatomy and sensory toolkit explain why sharks have remained among the ocean's most successful predators for hundreds of millions of years.
